Kelly LaBuff

Engineer / Designer / Product Manager

Problem

Resilient but fatigued farmers, with expansive barren fields need a means to make fertilizer adoption feasible, in order to improve their yield and free their families from the pervasive burden of hunger.

Super Semoir was a project incubated in the Stanford Design for Extreme Affordability class. In this class, our team was focused on designing and manufacturing a tool to enable extremely poor farmers in West Africa to adopt more efficient fertilization methods.

Process

  • Research and needfinding
  • Sketching and brainstorming around many whiteboards
  • Prototyping in cardboard, paper, metal, and wood
  • Refining prototypes with CAD, laser cutting, and welding
  • TESTING!
  • Iterate & repeat!

Tools

  • Rapid prototyping in machine shop: welding, woodworking, laser cutter
  • Solidworks
  • Grit
  • Teamwork

Outcome

  • Designed, and manufactured a functional multi-row microdose seeder prototype that can be pulled by a horse or cow
  • Increased seeding output by a factor of 2 compared to current South African market equivalent seeders

Our team also received funding from the Stanford Graduate School of Business to attempt to launch our product into a startup company.

Looking Forward

This project was life changing, and full of twists and turns. It sparked a passion for social impact and using design and technology for good in my career. And although our product development and design cycle was successful, other unforeseen business and global factors derailed our project.

This ultimately pushed me into the world of tech consulting at Accenture, to enable me to have both the technical and design background needed to develop a product, but the business acumen to get it to market successfully.
